## Problem

Let $f:[0,1] \to [0,\infty)$ be strictly increasing and continuous. Let $R$ be the region bounded by $x = 0$, $x = 1$, $y = 0$, and $y = f(x)$.

Let $x_1$ be the $x$-coordinate of the centroid of $R$. Let $x_2$ be the $x$-coordinate of the **centroid of the solid** generated by rotating $R$ about the $x$-axis.

Prove that $x_1 < x_2$.

## Submission

Let $f$ be a function such that $f: [0,1] \to [0, \infty)$ is continuous and strictly increasing.

**1. Definitions of the Centroids**

First, let us define the coordinates $x_1$ and $x_2$.

The area of the region $R$ is given by $A = \int_0^1 f(x) \, dx$.
The $x$-coordinate of the centroid of the region $R$, denoted $x_1$, is given by the formula:
$$ x_1 = \frac{\int_0^1 x f(x) \, dx}{\int_0^1 f(x) \, dx} $$
The volume of the solid generated by rotating $R$ about the $x$-axis is given by $V = \int_0^1 \pi (f(x))^2 \, dx$.
The moment of this solid about the plane $x=0$ is $M = \int_0^1 x \pi (f(x))^2 \, dx$.
The $x$-coordinate of the centroid of the solid, denoted $x_2$, is given by:
$$ x_2 = \frac{M}{V} = \frac{\int_0^1 x (f(x))^2 \, dx}{\int_0^1 (f(x))^2 \, dx} $$
Note that $\pi$ cancels out in the expression for $x_2$.

Since $f$ is strictly increasing and $f \ge 0$, and the domain is $[0,1]$, $f(x) > 0$ for all $x \in (0,1]$. Thus, the denominators $\int_0^1 f(x) \, dx$ and $\int_0^1 (f(x))^2 \, dx$ are strictly positive, so $x_1$ and $x_2$ are well-defined.

**2. Comparison of $x_1$ and $x_2$**

We wish to prove that $x_1 < x_2$.
$$ \frac{\int_0^1 x f(x) \, dx}{\int_0^1 f(x) \, dx} < \frac{\int_0^1 x (f(x))^2 \, dx}{\int_0^1 (f(x))^2 \, dx} $$
Since the denominators are positive, this is equivalent to proving:
$$ \left( \int_0^1 x f(x) \, dx \right) \left( \int_0^1 (f(x))^2 \, dx \right) < \left( \int_0^1 x (f(x))^2 \, dx \right) \left( \int_0^1 f(x) \, dx \right) $$
Let us define the difference $D$:
$$ D = \left( \int_0^1 x (f(x))^2 \, dx \right) \left( \int_0^1 f(x) \, dx \right) - \left( \int_0^1 x f(x) \, dx \right) \left( \int_0^1 (f(x))^2 \, dx \right) $$
We want to show that $D > 0$.

**3. Double Integral Representation**

We can express the products of integrals as double integrals over the square $[0,1] \times [0,1]$. Let the integration variables be $x$ and $y$.
$$ \left( \int_0^1 x (f(x))^2 \, dx \right) \left( \int_0^1 f(y) \, dy \right) = \int_0^1 \int_0^1 x (f(x))^2 f(y) \, dx \, dy $$
$$ \left( \int_0^1 x f(x) \, dx \right) \left( \int_0^1 (f(y))^2 \, dy \right) = \int_0^1 \int_0^1 x f(x) (f(y))^2 \, dx \, dy $$
Substituting these into the expression for $D$:
$$ D = \int_0^1 \int_0^1 \left[ x (f(x))^2 f(y) - x f(x) (f(y))^2 \right] \, dx \, dy $$
$$ D = \int_0^1 \int_0^1 x f(x) f(y) [ f(x) - f(y) ] \, dx \, dy $$
Now, we can exchange the variable names $x$ and $y$ without changing the value of the integral:
$$ D = \int_0^1 \int_0^1 y f(y) f(x) [ f(y) - f(x) ] \, dy \, dx $$
Notice that $[f(y) - f(x)] = -[f(x) - f(y)]$. So:
$$ D = \int_0^1 \int_0^1 -y f(x) f(y) [ f(x) - f(y) ] \, dx \, dy $$
Adding the two expressions for $D$:
$$ 2D = \int_0^1 \int_0^1 x f(x) f(y) [ f(x) - f(y) ] \, dx \, dy + \int_0^1 \int_0^1 -y f(x) f(y) [ f(x) - f(y) ] \, dx \, dy $$
$$ 2D = \int_0^1 \int_0^1 (x - y) f(x) f(y) [ f(x) - f(y) ] \, dx \, dy $$

**4. Analysis of the Sign**

Consider the integrand $g(x, y) = f(x) f(y) (x - y) (f(x) - f(y))$.
We analyze its sign on the domain $(0,1] \times (0,1]$:
1.  **Strictly Increasing Property**: Since $f$ is strictly increasing, for any $x \neq y$:
    *   If $x > y$, then $f(x) > f(y)$, so $(x - y) > 0$ and $(f(x) - f(y)) > 0$. The product $(x - y)(f(x) - f(y))$ is positive.
    *   If $x < y$, then $f(x) < f(y)$, so $(x - y) < 0$ and $(f(x) - f(y)) < 0$. The product $(x - y)(f(x) - f(y))$ is positive.
    Thus, $(x - y)(f(x) - f(y)) > 0$ for all $x \neq y$.

2.  **Positivity of $f$**: Since $f$ is strictly increasing on $[0,1]$ into $[0, \infty)$, $f(x) > f(0) \ge 0$ for all $x > 0$.
    Therefore, the term $f(x)f(y)$ is strictly positive for all $x, y \in (0, 1]$.

Combining these, the integrand $f(x) f(y) (x - y) (f(x) - f(y))$ is strictly positive for all $(x, y) \in (0, 1] \times (0, 1]$ such that $x \neq y$.
The set of points where $x = y$ (the diagonal) or where $x=0$ or $y=0$ has measure zero in the integration domain $[0,1] \times [0,1]$.
Since the integrand is continuous, non-negative, and strictly positive almost everywhere, the definite integral is strictly positive.

$$ 2D > 0 \implies D > 0 $$

**5. Conclusion**

Since $D > 0$, we have:
$$ \left( \int_0^1 x (f(x))^2 \, dx \right) \left( \int_0^1 f(x) \, dx \right) > \left( \int_0^1 x f(x) \, dx \right) \left( \int_0^1 (f(x))^2 \, dx \right) $$
Dividing both sides by the positive product of the denominators:
$$ \frac{\int_0^1 x (f(x))^2 \, dx}{\int_0^1 (f(x))^2 \, dx} > \frac{\int_0^1 x f(x) \, dx}{\int_0^1 f(x) \, dx} $$
$$ x_2 > x_1 $$
Thus, $x_1 < x_2$.

Q.E.D.
